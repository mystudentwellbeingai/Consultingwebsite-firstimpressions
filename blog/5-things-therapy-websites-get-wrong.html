5 Things Your Therapy Website Gets Wrong

I've reviewed a lot of therapist and counselor websites. And while every practice is different, the same five problems show up again and again. These aren't design flaws or technical bugs. They're messaging problems that quietly push potential clients away before they ever reach out.

1. Leading with credentials instead of connection

Your homepage opens with "I'm a Registered Psychotherapist with a Master's in Counseling Psychology, specializing in CBT, DBT, and EMDR." That's great for a professional directory. But for someone Googling "therapist near me" at 11pm, it reads like a resume.

What they want to see first is that you understand what they're going through. Credentials matter, but they belong further down the page, after you've made a connection.

2. Using clinical language your clients don't speak

"Strength-based approach." "Evidence-based modalities." "Psychodynamic framework." These terms are meaningful to other professionals. To someone considering therapy for the first time, they're noise.

When you write "I use a strength-based approach," try "I help you build on what's already working in your life." Same idea, completely different feeling.

3. Writing about yourself instead of your client

Count how many times your homepage says "I" versus "you." If it's heavily weighted toward "I," your site is talking about yourself rather than talking to the person reading it. Your visitors aren't there to learn your story. They're there to find out if you can help with theirs.

4. Burying the contact information

If someone has to scroll through three pages to find out how to book a session, you've lost them. Your phone number, email, or booking link should be visible on every single page. The moment someone decides "this might be the right therapist," you need to make the next step effortless.

5. Ignoring what the site feels like

Your website has a mood, whether you designed it that way or not. Heavy blocks of text, harsh colors, and stock photos of sunsets create a feeling. Often that feeling is "this was built quickly and never revisited." For someone who's already anxious about reaching out, the visual environment of your site matters more than you think.

The good news

None of these are expensive to fix. Most of them just require looking at your site through the eyes of someone who has never heard of you and asking: "Would I feel comfortable reaching out to this person?"

That's exactly what I do in a website audit. If any of these sound familiar, it might be worth a conversation.
